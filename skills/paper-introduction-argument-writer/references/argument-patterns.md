# Introduction Argument Patterns

Choose one primary pattern. Do not mix several full patterns unless the paper is genuinely hybrid.

## Method Breakthrough

Best for papers selling a new method or algorithm.

Paragraph sequence:

1. Problem importance and current capability frontier.
2. Specific limitation of existing methods.
3. Core insight that makes the proposed method plausible.
4. Method overview at the level of mechanism, not implementation.
5. Evidence summary showing the method works.
6. Contributions.

Writing pressure:

- The gap must make the method necessary.
- The insight should be stated before the method name becomes prominent.
- The result paragraph should support the mechanism claim, not only report benchmark wins.

## Benchmark or Dataset

Best for papers selling an evaluation resource, dataset, task suite, or protocol.

Paragraph sequence:

1. Field progress depends on a capability that is not measured well.
2. Existing benchmarks or datasets miss a key axis.
3. Design principles for the new benchmark or dataset.
4. What the resource contains and how it is evaluated.
5. Findings revealed by the resource.
6. Contributions and release promise.

Writing pressure:

- Sell the evaluation gap before the resource.
- Results should include findings, not only dataset statistics.
- Avoid claiming community adoption before evidence exists.

## Empirical Study

Best for papers selling controlled evidence, taxonomy, or measurement.

Paragraph sequence:

1. Common practice or belief in the field.
2. Why existing evidence is incomplete, confounded, or anecdotal.
3. Controlled study design or analysis lens.
4. Main findings.
5. Implications for researchers or practitioners.
6. Contributions.

Writing pressure:

- The contribution is the clarified evidence, not a new method.
- Avoid turning findings into universal laws unless the design supports it.

## Analysis or Diagnostic

Best for papers explaining why a phenomenon happens.

Paragraph sequence:

1. Phenomenon that affects model behavior or evaluation.
2. Existing explanations are insufficient or conflicting.
3. Diagnostic framework or decomposition.
4. Key analysis findings.
5. Consequences for method design or evaluation.
6. Contributions.

Writing pressure:

- Define the phenomenon concretely.
- Keep causal language proportional to the analysis design.

## Systems or Tooling

Best for papers selling practical scale, efficiency, deployment, or infrastructure.

Paragraph sequence:

1. Practical bottleneck at current scale or workflow.
2. Why straightforward engineering or existing systems are insufficient.
3. Design principles.
4. System overview.
5. Scale, speed, cost, or usability evidence.
6. Contributions and artifact.

Writing pressure:

- Quantify the bottleneck when possible.
- Connect design choices to measured system properties.

## Application

Best for domain-driven ML/AI papers.

Paragraph sequence:

1. Domain problem and why it matters.
2. Why existing general methods or domain approaches fall short.
3. Domain-specific insight or constraint.
4. Proposed approach.
5. Domain-relevant results.
6. Contributions.

Writing pressure:

- Use domain metrics and constraints, not only generic ML metrics.
- Avoid claiming broad ML novelty if the main value is domain impact.

## Negative Result or Limitation

Best for papers selling a boundary, failure mode, or cautionary result.

Paragraph sequence:

1. Widely used assumption, method, benchmark, or practice.
2. Why the assumption matters.
3. Stress test or analysis design.
4. Negative or limiting finding.
5. Scope and constructive recommendation.
6. Contributions.

Writing pressure:

- Write confidently about evidence, carefully about generality.
- Make the constructive value clear.
